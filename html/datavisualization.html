Data Visualization

Spring 2024

Introduction

This was a two person project completed over the course of about 5 weeks. Together, we created a series of data visualizations to explore effects of social media on mental health. We created these visuals using Vega-Lite in Observable. A full report can be found here →. In this report you will find detailed information on the process of making the visuals, our findings, and be able to interact with the visuals in real time.

Process

We started by defining 6 questions that would guide our exploration and serve as jumping points for creating our visualizations. For example: What is the relationship between the number of hours using social media and poor mental health tendencies? After defining these questions, we set out on sketching potential visualizations. We did this mostly on paper, but I also tried using Tableau to quickly generate visualizations to see how effective they were.

We wanted to keep our visuals as simple as possible. Data can often seem overwhelming, so we wanted to make the experience intuitive. One way we achieved this was through interactivity.

We grouped visuals of similar kinds together with a toggle to switch between variables. The heat maps below are one example. They show the relationship between respondent’s daily social media usage and their tendency to get distracted by social media, sleeping issues, and ability to concentrate. The interactivity here allows us to condense 3 visuals into one, effectively saving space while still allowing the user to see the relationships between the visuals and analyze the trends.

Another visual that had interactive elements was this histogram showing the distribution of ages of survey respondents for the first survey. A toggle was used again to filter between either respondents’ gender or occupation. The majority of respondents were between the ages of 18 and 27, but there were a few odd entries up to age 91. Instead of getting rid of these outliers completely, we give the user the ability to filter them out using this slider if they want to. Reducing the range of ages shown also makes it easier to compare to the age and gender histogram for the other survey.

One final way we implemented interactivity was through “brushing.” The scatterplot below shows respondent's tendency to compare themselves to others through social media (self esteem). After selecting a range of responses on the left scatter plot the user can see their corresponding ages in the visual on the right. This helps separate the data amongst different ages to identify whether one age range suffers more significantly than the other. A scatterplot, like a heat map, allows us to see the relationship between two dimensions. The use of colour here acts the same as a heat map as well, telling us how many cases are stacked on top of one another. We preferred a scatterplot in this case though because the age data is much more widespread. This visual helped us notice that the majority of people who ranked 4 or 5 on the self esteem scale were in their early twenties, which could make sense considering that the environmental factors of these university students may be impacting their social media usage and self esteem.

One of the most shocking things we found in our exploration of the data was the dramatic difference in effects of social media on each gender. In the visuals below, you can see that women tend to be more at risk for lower self esteem and addictive social media tendencies than men.

Reflection

During my co-op with Fraser Health Authority I learned a lot about designing good data visuals, but I didn’t learn how to actually work with data and create the visuals using code. Being able to implement the knowledge I gained during my work semester to create my own designs from start to finish in this project was very satisfying. I also really appreciated learning the technical rules around designing data formally to solidify my knowledge. I definitely have a newfound appreciation for data as a whole after learning its complexities. There are so many ways to display the same information, but it takes a lot of thought and care to pick the right way for specific use cases. I am positive that the skills I have developed for designing data will prove useful for a long time.
