Scale Model

Summer 2023

Introduction

This was a 3 person group project. Our diorama is an abandoned and overgrown graveyard scene, comprised of flocking, painted foam, and paper mache. It feature little glowing LED ghosts as a form of lighting. We chose this concept as we felt it allowed for a variety of colors, materials and general creativity without requiring excessive precision or being otherwise unrealistic to build.

Process

Before building anything, a rough model was quickly created in Blender. Though not used closely as a reference, this allowed us to evaluate and plan placement and scale, especially in height.

Using techniques we learned in class, stone structures were created out of dry floral foam with indentations made with a pen, and additional texture added using tinfoil. Small pieces of thin plastic were used as windows, and several bricks were removed to enhance its aged feel. The material proved somewhat challenging to work with being prone to crumble and difficult to glue. As such, several pieces had to be remade or have additional pieces retrofitted to fix mistakes. Despite its problems, we chose this foam as it had an especially appropriate texture and colour for replicating stone.

Our trees also built off of techniques learned in class. They were created by twisting twine covered wire and splitting pieces off to create branches, where flocking was glued on. The early iterations were too tall and not full enough, so we shortened them and created more low branches.

The next step was to test the lighting feature and create a box to act as a base for the diorama and conseal the electroncis. The ghost lights were made using hot glue over white lEDs. We later used sharpie to add small eyes.

We created the box using laser cut particle board. This required some planning because we needed to cut out holes for the ghosts.

Once the base was cut out, we could begin assembling the hill for our graveyard. We used floral foam pieces to create a rough landscape, and then covered it with paper mache. This took a very long time to dry, so we attempted to speed up the process using a heat gun.

All that was left was to paint and assemble. We painted the base, church, and gravestones. After everything was dry and glued into place on the base, we covered the grassy areas in flocking for a more realistic texture.

Challenges

A major challenge we faced was with the paper mache base. The paper mache contracted as it died, which caused enough shrinkage to rip the foam it was wrapped around from the box it was glued on. We tried to reglue it, but there were still areas with a crack between the paper mache and the base. We managed to conseal these holes using tape painted green and covered in flocking.

One small challenge we faced was with the button for the ghost lights. The button was difficult to position in a way that would stay in place when pressed. We managed to fit the button in perfectly with a piece of foam secured in place with glue.

We also recieved critiques from our professor that he would like the laser cut box to be hidden. This was not part of our plan, so we decided to extend the hillside using pieces of foam to hide the box. We made one side of the hill removable so that we could have access to inside the box for the electronics.

Reflection

In the end, we finished this project in a timely manner and delegated work effectively. Our plan to split the project into smaller components was an effective strategy for our team of 3 people to contribute asynchronously.

One thing we noticed was that prior knowledge of crafts and working with materials was uneven in the team, which led to an uneven distribution of work. While we did expect this to happen, the less experienced members felt that they could have spent more time learning general crafting techniques to improve their own skills and contribute more.

Things we learned about the materials:

  • Floral foam proved malleable, but somewhat imprecise and difficult to work with, being highly porous and breaking easily. While well-suited to this project, it’s likely poor for anything larger.
  • We used hot glue, super glue, and plain white glue for this project. Hot glue seems best for large, highly stressed objects, whereas super glue felt suited for securing small, sturdy things (such as the headstones) and white glue worked well for securing flocking to a large surface.
  • Paper maché proved effective for creating a smooth, workable surface from a rough set of underlying parts. However, while not time consuming to do, it takes a long time to dry. As previously mentioned, it also contracted when it dried which caused the foam to lift away from our base. This could be prevented by more securely glueing the foam, or by using a different material like chickenwire to create our hill shape.
